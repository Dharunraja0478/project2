Voiced Sounds

Voiced sounds, also known as voiced consonants or sonorants, are a class of speech sounds produced with the vocal cords vibrating. In contrast to voiceless sounds, where the vocal cords are not engaged and there is no vibration, voiced sounds involve the vibration of the vocal cords during articulation. This vibration creates a fundamental frequency that gives voiced sounds their characteristic quality and distinguishes them from voiceless sounds.

Articulation:

Voiced sounds are produced by bringing the vocal cords together, allowing air to pass through them. The articulators, such as the tongue, lips, and teeth, shape the airflow to produce different voiced sounds. For example, the tongue position for producing a voiced 'z' sound is similar to that for producing the voiceless 's' sound, but the vocal cords vibrate during the production of 'z'.

Vocal Cord Vibration:

The key characteristic of voiced sounds is the vibration of the vocal cords. When the vocal cords are brought close together and air from the lungs passes through them, they vibrate. This vibration produces a buzzing or humming quality in the sound.

Sound Spectrum::

The vibration of the vocal cords generates a fundamental frequency and harmonics, which together create the sound spectrum of the voiced sound. The fundamental frequency corresponds to the perceived pitch of the sound, while the harmonics contribute to its timbre.

Examples of Voiced Sounds:

Examples of voiced consonants in English include /b/, /d/, /g/, /v/, /z/, /ʒ/ (as in "measure"), /ð/ (as in "this"), and /m/, /n/, /ŋ/ (nasal consonants). Voiced sounds are also found in vowels, where the vocal cords vibrate continuously during their production.

Voicing Contrasts:

In many languages, including English, the distinction between voiced and voiceless sounds is phonemic, meaning that it can change the meaning of words. For example, the difference between the words "pat" /pæt/ and "bat" /bæt/ lies in the voicing of the initial consonant.

Voiced Consonant Pairs:

Some languages have pairs of consonants that are identical in terms of their place and manner of articulation but differ only in voicing. For example, in English, the pair /p/ and /b/ are both bilabial plosives, but /p/ is voiceless, while /b/ is voiced.
