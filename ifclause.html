If clause

An "if clause" is a type of conditional sentence in English grammar. Conditional sentences express a hypothetical or imagined situation and its potential consequence. There are three main types of conditional sentences, each with its own structure and usage. The "if clause" is a crucial part of these sentences and is often referred to as the conditional clause.

Zero Conditional (Real Conditional):

Used for general truths, facts, or real situations that are always true.

Structure: If + present simple, present simple.

Example: If it rains, the streets get wet.


First Conditional (Future Real Conditional):

Used for possible or likely future situations and their potential outcomes.

Structure: If + present simple, will + base form of the verb.

Example: If it rains tomorrow, we will stay indoors.


Second Conditional (Unreal Conditional - Present or Future):

Used for unreal or unlikely present or future situations and their hypothetical outcomes.

Structure: If + past simple, would + base form of the verb.

Example: If I won the lottery, I would travel around the world.


Third Conditional (Unreal Conditional - Past):

Used for unreal or hypothetical past situations and their imagined outcomes.

Structure: If + past perfect, would have + past participle.

Example: If he had studied harder, he would have passed the exam.



In each case, the "if clause" introduces the condition or situation upon which the outcome depends. The main clause follows and expresses the result or consequence of that condition. It's important to note that while the "if clause" can come first in the sentence, the order can be reversed without changing the meaning.

Example: If it rains tomorrow, we will stay indoors.

We will stay indoors if it rains tomorrow.


Additionally, it's possible to omit the "if" clause in a conditional sentence, especially in informal or conversational English.

Example: I would travel around the world if I won the lottery.

I would have passed the exam if he had studied harder.
