Degrees of Comparision

The degrees of comparison in English grammar refer to the forms of adjectives and adverbs that indicate the extent, intensity, or relative quality of a particular characteristic. There are three degrees of comparison: positive, comparative, and superlative.

Positive Degree:

This is the basic form of an adjective or adverb, used to describe a noun or a verb without making any comparison.

Example: Positive (adjective): The cat is cute.

Positive (adverb): She sings beautifully.



Comparative Degree:

This degree is used when comparing two things or actions. For adjectives, the comparative form is usually created by adding "-er" to the positive form. For adverbs, "more" is often added before the adverb.

Example: Comparative (adjective): The dog is cuter than the cat.

Comparative (adverb): She sings more beautifully than her sister.



Superlative Degree:

This degree is used when comparing three or more things or actions. For adjectives, the superlative form is usually created by adding "-est" to the positive form. For adverbs, "most" is often added before the adverb.

Example: Superlative (adjective): The elephant is the cutest in the zoo.

Superlative (adverb): She sings most beautifully of all.



It's important to note some irregular forms and exceptions when forming comparatives and superlatives:


Irregular adjectives and adverbs don't follow the standard rules. For example, "good" becomes "better" in the comparative and "best" in the superlative.


Some adjectives and adverbs use "more" and "most" instead of adding suffixes. For instance, "beautiful" becomes "more beautiful" in the comparative and "most beautiful" in the superlative.


Examples of irregular forms:

Positive: good, bad, far

Comparative: better, worse, farther/further

Superlative: best, worst, farthest/furthest
